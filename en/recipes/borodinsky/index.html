R2-C1 is an advanced regional rye lesson. Borodinsky is the classic dark Russian scalded rye: coriander, honey, red rye malt, rye sourdough, and sweet-sour balance.

What This Lesson Studies

The main topic is scalded rye as a flavor system. This lesson asks how sourdough acidity, malt and honey sweetness, coriander aroma, crumb moisture, and post-bake rest work together.

Theory

Borodinsky should not be the first rye lesson. It contains many variables at once: rye sourdough, scald, malt, honey, coriander, high moisture, pan baking, storage, and crumb rest. That is why it belongs in R2 after the R1 fundamentals. The key controls are starter maturity, scald holding time, proof height, internal temperature, weight loss, storage, and crumb at 12–24 h.

Full course map, currently in Russian: Учебная программа хлебной лаборатории.

Sensory Card

After resting, record acidity, sweetness, malt note, coriander aroma, moisture, knife stickiness, density, doughy mouthfeel, and aftertaste. A score is useful only together with a reason: airiness, acidity, salt, spice, crust, or storage.

Lesson Conclusion

This is the third public iteration. The next strong test should separate technological crumb moisture from storage effects. Do not change water or sweetness automatically before the crumb and storage data are collected.

Instructions

  1. Scald

    Red rye malt + 100 ml boiling water. Rest 3–12 hours at room temperature.

  2. Mix

    Combine flours, water, starter, scald, salt, honey, and coriander. Mix on speed 1–3 for 5 minutes.

  3. Bulk ferment

    8–10 hours at 22–24 °C. Volume increases 1.5–2 times.

  4. Shape and proof

    Shape and place in a pan. Proof 1.5–2 hours until the dough rises 1.5 cm above the pan rim.

  5. Bake

    250 °C for 10 minutes, then 200 °C for 75 minutes. Internal crumb temperature ≥ 98 °C.

For readers who want to understand why the formula changed.

How the Borodinsky formula changed

The research section records not only the final formula, but the working trail: what went wrong, which solutions were possible, what was chosen, and what evidence supports it.

Base version

The base version was a starting point: rye sourdough bread with coriander, honey, and malt. The main risk is making something that looks like Borodinsky without verifiable data on flavour, crumb moisture, fermentation, and scald timing. Every new iteration therefore needs a trace: photos, actual time, temperature, and a short conclusion.

Iteration analysis

01 Scald: the 3–12 hour range needs actual data
What went wrong
The recipe has a working scald range, but without actual time and temperature it is impossible to know which hold produced the specific flavour and colour.
Observation
In the 2026-04-25 photo the scald/pre-ferment looks uniform and ready to mix in, but the photo alone does not answer how long it stood or at what temperature.
Hypothesis
Red rye malt depends not only on boiling water and mixing, but also on hold time: too short may give less depth, too long may change acidity and aroma.
Decision and why
The third option is chosen: the range remains, but the next bake must produce an actual record. Without it, the conclusion about best hold time is weak.
What changed
The next-bake timeline now records scald time, kitchen temperature, and a photo before mixing.
Result
The scald remains mandatory, but its effect is not fully measured yet.
Conclusion
A stronger conclusion needs photo + hold time + temperature + tasting.
Options considered
  • Fix a minimum 3 hour hold and keep the process simple.
  • Hold for 12 hours as an overnight stage.
  • Keep the range for now, but record each bake as an experiment: time, temperature, aroma, photo.

02 Cold pause: useful for scheduling, not yet proven as part of the final recipe
What went wrong
The journal has photos of dough before and after the fridge. That matters, but it should not become the final recipe without crumb and flavour evidence.
Observation
The photos show that the dough holds shape and changes surface after the cold pause. There is no connected crumb photo or tasting note yet.
Hypothesis
A cold pause may help scheduling and slow fermentation, but in dough with a high proportion of rye flour it may also increase stickiness or reduce rise if started too early.
Decision and why
The third option is chosen: the final recipe keeps room-temperature fermentation, while the cold pause is documented as a testable branch.
What changed
The timeline now says that if the fridge is used, before/after photos, actual time, and crumb photos are required.
Result
The cold pause is neither hidden nor discarded: it is framed as a hypothesis for the next bake.
Conclusion
For public readers this is more honest than declaring the fridge mandatory without proof.
Options considered
  • Remove the fridge from the final recipe and keep fermentation at 22–24 °C.
  • Make the fridge a required stage.
  • Keep the cold pause as a research branch until crumb photos and tasting exist.

03 Proofing: dough height matters more than time alone
What went wrong
The phrase “1.5–2 hours” is convenient, but for pan rye it can mislead: different temperature or starter activity shifts readiness.
Observation
The photo of dough in the pan captures a real process state. That is more useful than time alone because it shows rise level and surface condition.
Hypothesis
Proofing should use two signals together: time as an estimate, height against the pan as the checkpoint.
Decision and why
The third option is chosen: the recipe keeps 1.5–2 hours, but the checkpoint is about 1.5 cm above the pan rim.
What changed
The next bake needs a side photo so the height is comparable between iterations.
Result
Proofing becomes repeatable by observed dough state, not only by a timer.
Conclusion
The next step is to record pan size, making photos and rise height comparable data.
Options considered
  • Use proofing time only.
  • Use visual state only.
  • Use time, height against the pan rim, and a side photo.

04 Finished loaf: exterior is documented, but the cut photo is still missing locally
What went wrong
A finished-loaf photo confirms shape and crust, but it does not answer the main rye-bread question: what happened to the crumb after full cooling.
Observation
The finished loaf looks formed, with a clear crust. User feedback from 27 Apr says the crumb was still wet, left residue on the knife, felt dough-like when chewed, and the taste score was 4/10.
Hypothesis
If the crumb is sticky after 24–48 hours, possible causes include sealed plastic storage, too much available moisture, fermentation point, short mixing, or a combination of those factors. Without a cut photo, visual structure cannot be separated from sensory feedback.
Decision and why
The third option is chosen: do not change water or sweetness before a controlled non-plastic storage check. The product goal is clearer: more open crumb, less obvious sweetness, and a more present but still moderate coriander/caraway aroma.
What changed
The next bake now requires storage in linen/cotton or a towel for the first 24–48 hours, crumb photos at 12 and 24 hours, and a knife-stickiness note.
Result
The finished loaf can be shown publicly, but the water and storage conclusion remains open.
Conclusion
The next strong iteration should separate two things: technological crumb wetness and the effect of sealed plastic storage.
Options considered
  • Call the recipe confirmed based on exterior.
  • Change water immediately.
  • First test storage and collect crumb photos at 12 and 24 hours.

Questions

How much salt?

10 g per 700 g loaf in version 3.0. Version 1.0 used 6 g — too little, flavour fell flat.

Can I skip the scald?

Technically yes, but the scald delivers the characteristic dark colour and flavour. Without it you get ordinary rye bread.
